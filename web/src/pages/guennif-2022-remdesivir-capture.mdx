## A pandemic drug gets a rare-disease label

At the height of the worst public health crisis in a century, a drug being actively tested against COVID-19 was quietly awarded a status designed to protect medicines for diseases that almost no one has. The reward attached to that label — exclusive market rights — was worth a fortune to a single company.

This maneuver is the subject of a **2022** analysis published in *Public Choice*, spanning pages **163 to 186** of volume **193**. The authors argue the episode reveals how pharmaceutical regulation can be bent not by criminals, but by a system of incentives and institutional pressures operating entirely within the law.

The paradox is stark: a globally spreading pandemic pathogen became the justification for a protection mechanism invented for the opposite situation — diseases so uncommon they attract no commercial investment without government subsidy.

<ScrollySection id="problem">
<div class="section-label">Section 02</div>

## Seven years of monopoly for a 'rare' disease

**Seven years** of market exclusivity — that is the reward the Orphan Drug Act promises to any company that wins approval for a treatment targeting a rare disease. The logic is sound in its original context: markets will not supply drugs for illnesses affecting very few patients, so a period of monopoly pricing offsets the commercial risk.

COVID-19 is the categorical opposite of rare. The disease infected populations across every continent simultaneously. Yet the designation was granted, and with it came the full statutory prize that Congress had designed for neglected patients who otherwise had nowhere to turn.

The authors' paper, received on **20 February 2021** and accepted on **10 September 2022**, frames this outcome as an analytic problem demanding a theory — not merely a scandal demanding outrage. Their question is structural: what conditions in the regulatory system allowed an obviously communicable, epidemic disease to qualify?

<div class="section-label">Section 03</div>

## Tracing one drug through the system

**A case study of the remdesivir episode is conducted**, combining a new analytical framework with a reconstruction of the drug's full regulatory biography. The authors do not rely on speculation about intent; instead they map observable facts — filings, approvals, personnel movements — against a typology of capture.

The framework's core claim is that regulatory capture is not a binary condition. It exists on a spectrum, and **the degree of capture determines the degree of predation** on the public interest. This graded model allows the authors to classify the remdesivir episode with precision, rather than simply asserting that something went wrong.

The study was published on **8 October 2022**, drawing on a record that by then extended across nearly two decades of pharmaceutical development. The **23-page** analysis sits at the intersection of public choice economics and health policy, carrying JEL codes I18, K23, and L12 — welfare economics, regulatory law, and market structure simultaneously.

## From Ebola failure to COVID candidate

Remdesivir was not invented for COVID-19. The molecule has a long, recycled history: it was synthesized originally to fight coronaviruses, then tried against Ebola, then repositioned again for SARS and MERS, before the pandemic gave it a third commercial life.

That trajectory — **the stylized facts of a repurposed drug** — is central to the orphan designation story. Because the molecule had already been developed and tested across multiple prior programs, the marginal investment required to pivot it toward COVID-19 was a fraction of what the seven-year exclusivity reward implies.

The authors trace this history not to impugn the science, but because repurposing economics expose the mismatch: a drug whose R&D costs had already been largely sunk across prior indications was positioned as though it were a fresh, high-risk bet on a neglected patient population.

<ScrollySection id="finding-capture">
<div class="section-label">Section 05</div>

## A chain of captures bends the rules

The designation was not a single corrupt decision — it was **the result of a series of captures**. Each link in the chain involved regulators facing pressure, information asymmetry, or institutional incentives that nudged outcomes toward the applicant's interest rather than the public's.

The authors classify this as **a case of weak capture**: no bribe, no explicit quid pro quo, no law broken. Instead, the mechanism is subtler. **Revolving doors are neither illegal nor condemned** — officials move between regulatory agencies and the industries they once supervised, carrying relationships and cognitive frameworks that tilt future decisions. That tilt, compounded across multiple decision points, is enough to produce predation without prosecution.

**The degree of capture determines the degree of predation**: at the weak end of the spectrum, the public pays through elevated prices and delayed competition; at the strong end, safety itself is compromised. The remdesivir episode lands firmly in the first category — costly, but not catastrophic. The authors' concern is that the same pathway, with slightly more pressure at each node, could produce worse outcomes in future emergencies.

<ScrollySection id="implication">
<div class="section-label">Section 06</div>

## Close the loophole before the next pandemic

The authors' prescription is direct: amend the Orphan Drug Act so that drugs targeting contagious, communicable, epidemic diseases are categorically ineligible for orphan status. The loophole is not ambiguous — it is a structural gap that rational actors will exploit again whenever incentives align.

Published in *Public Choice* volume **193** in **2022**, this analysis arrives as governments around the world were drawing lessons from the COVID-19 response. The remdesivir episode is the authors' evidence that regulatory reform cannot wait for the next emergency to reveal its costs — by then, the capture will already have occurred.

The broader warning is institutional. If the protections designed for the rarest, most neglected patients can be redirected toward blockbuster pandemic drugs, then the entire framework of orphan drug policy is vulnerable to the same mechanism. The fix requires not just closing this specific loophole, but building into regulatory design an explicit check against epidemic and pandemic pathogens — before the next novel virus tests the system again.
