## The Hidden Payoff Inside a Licensing Deal

When a pharma company licenses out its technology, the textbook story is simple: it hands over knowledge for cash. But a **2022 study in *Technovation*** (vol. 112, article 102405) argues the knowledge can flow backwards too — the licensor quietly learns from the partner it sold to. The question: does selling your technology actually teach you something?

The answer, it turns out, is yes. Drawing on **1,861 licensing deals** involving **254 pharmaceutical and biotech firms** active between **1995 and 2015**, the authors provide the first rigorous empirical test of what they call "reverse learning" — a channel that has been hiding in plain sight inside every out-licensing agreement.

The paper was available online as early as **16 October 2021**, published in *Technovation* under Elsevier's umbrella (ISSN 0166-4972), and sits at the intersection of innovation management and technology strategy.

<div class="data-source-tag">Source: Kelchtermans et al., Technovation 112 (2022) 102405; Available online 16 October 2021, Elsevier Ltd.</div>
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="problem">
<div class="section-label">Section 02</div>

## Licensing Exploded — But Who Benefits?

Over the last few decades, licensing has become a dominant mechanism for transferring technology between firms. The authors note that this growth has been particularly pronounced since the early 2000s — with high-tech industries such as semiconductors, chemicals, electronics, pharmaceuticals, and biotech all deepening their reliance on licensing as a core strategic tool.

Within pharma and biotech specifically, companies **frequently engage in out-licensing to capitalize on their R&D efforts** — turning internal discoveries into revenue streams by selling access to external partners. Yet virtually all prior research has focused on what the *buyer* gains from these arrangements, leaving a significant blind spot on the seller's side.

The dataset the authors assembled — **1,861 deals** across **254 firms** spanning **1995 to 2015** — is large enough to finally close that gap. For the first time, the licensor is the subject, not the background character.

<div class="data-source-tag">Source: Kelchtermans et al., Technovation 112 (2022) 102405, Introduction; dataset spanning 1995–2015.</div>
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="method">
<div class="section-label">Section 03</div>

## 1,861 Deals, Two Decades of Data

The empirical backbone of this study is formidable: **1,861 licensing deals** drawn from **254 pharmaceutical and biotech firms**, tracked across a **20-year window from 1995 to 2015**. Each deal represents a dyad — a licensor paired with a licensee — and the authors track what happens to knowledge flows inside those relationships after the ink dries.

The key measurement instrument is the patent citation. When a licensor's subsequent patents begin citing the patents of its licensee, that backward citation leaves a traceable fingerprint of knowledge flowing in the "wrong" direction — from buyer back to seller. Patent data were sourced from the **PATSTAT database (version May 2018)**, one of the most comprehensive global patent repositories available for this kind of longitudinal analysis.

By anchoring the learning signal in observable, verifiable citation records rather than survey self-reports, the authors sidestep a perennial problem in knowledge-spillover research: the fact that firms rarely know — or admit — exactly where their ideas came from.

<div class="data-source-tag">Source: Kelchtermans et al., Technovation 112 (2022) 102405; PATSTAT database, version May 2018; dataset: 1,861 deals, 254 firms, 1995–2015.</div>
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="players">
<div class="section-label">Section 04</div>

## The Giants Behind the Deals

The licensing market in this dataset was heavily concentrated among a handful of industry titans. On the licensor side, **Roche** topped the table with **80 deals**, followed by **Crucell** with **68** and **Elan** with **58** — firms whose intellectual property portfolios were rich enough to attract a steady stream of buyers across two decades.

The buyer side tells an equally concentrated story. **Pfizer** led all licensees with **139 deals**, ahead of **Novartis** at **133** and **Roche** again at **127**. That Roche appears prominently on both lists is not an anomaly — it illustrates a central feature of the pharma landscape: the same firms routinely play buyer *and* seller, depending on the technology and the moment.

This dual-role behavior has a direct implication for the paper's thesis. If the same firms switch positions depending on the deal, then the learning flowing "backwards" from licensee to licensor is not a niche edge case — it is woven into the ordinary fabric of how the industry exchanges knowledge.

<div class="data-source-tag">Source: Kelchtermans et al., Technovation 112 (2022) 102405, Table: Overview of 20 largest licensors and licensees by number of licensing deals.</div>
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="finding">
<div class="section-label">Section 05</div>

## Sellers Learn More Than Twice as Much

The central finding lands with force: a licensor makes on average **3.49 citations** to its licensee's patents — **more than twice the rate** observed between two firms that share no licensing relationship. This is the smoking gun for reverse learning, measured directly in the patent record rather than inferred from surveys or proxies.

What makes the number striking is not just the magnitude but the direction. Economic intuition suggests that technology flows from seller to buyer — the licensor teaches, the licensee absorbs. Yet the citation data reveal a mirror image: the seller is watching what the buyer does with the technology, and it is learning from what it sees.

After applying rigorous statistical controls to rule out alternative explanations — firm size, technological proximity, industry trends — the gap holds. The authors describe this as the **first clear empirical test** that reverse learning by the licensor is a real, measurable phenomenon in the pharmaceutical industry.

<div class="data-source-tag">Source: Kelchtermans et al., Technovation 112 (2022) 102405; average citation rate of 3.49 from licensor to licensee patents.</div>
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="rigor">
<div class="section-label">Section 06</div>

## Comparing Apples to Apples

A perennial concern in studies of this kind is that large firms simply cite more patents — not because they are learning from a partner, but because their sheer size generates more citation activity. The authors address this head-on using **coarsened exact matching (CEM)**, a technique that constructs a comparison group of non-licensing firm pairs with statistically equivalent observable characteristics.

The scale of the matching exercise is revealing: the raw pool contained **865,401 non-licensing dyads**. After CEM, that pool was trimmed to a precisely balanced **1,608-vs-1,608 comparison** — licensing pairs matched against near-identical non-licensing pairs.

The critical diagnostic is the patent-stock gap between the two groups. Before matching, licensing firms had a mean patent-stock difference of **883.60** relative to the control group — a chasm that could easily confound the results. After matching, that gap collapsed to just **6.39**. With firm size and technological endowment neutralized, what remains is the learning effect itself: the signal that out-licensing, and not simply being big, drives the reverse citation pattern.

<div class="data-source-tag">Source: Kelchtermans et al., Technovation 112 (2022) 102405; CEM results: 865,401 raw dyads → 1,608/1,608 matched; Δ patent stock 883.60 (pre-matching) → 6.39 (post-matching).</div>
</ScrollySection>

<ScrollySection id="implication">
<div class="section-label">Section 07</div>

## Selling Tech Can Make You Smarter

The takeaway from this study fundamentally reframes how managers should think about out-licensing. For decades, the conventional wisdom has treated licensing as a one-directional transaction: you sell access to your R&D, you collect royalties, and the knowledge leaves the building. The **1,861-deal, 20-year** dataset assembled here tells a different story.

Out-licensing opens a channel. Once a licensee has your technology in hand, it starts doing things with it — filing patents, making improvements, exploring adjacent applications. The licensor, if it stays alert, can observe those developments through patent citations and absorb them. The deal you signed to *sell* technology may quietly upgrade your own innovation engine.

For pharmaceutical and biotech firms that already **engage in out-licensing to capitalize on their R&D efforts**, this finding adds a second line item to the strategic calculus. The financial return from a licensing deal is visible and immediate. The learning return — measured here in the reverse flow of **254 firms'** citation behavior across two decades — is quieter, slower, and until now, almost entirely overlooked.

<div class="data-source-tag">Source: Kelchtermans et al., Technovation 112 (2022) 102405; 1,861 licensing deals, 254 pharmaceutical and biotech firms, 1995–2015; reverse learning finding and out-licensing strategy discussion.</div>
